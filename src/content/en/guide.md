LoiClair is a citizen dashboard that makes French legislative activity readable and accessible to everyone, without any prior expertise. This guide presents all the pages of the site and explains how to use them.

---

## Fil d'actualité

The **News feed** (Fil d'actualité) page presents legislative activity as a chronological feed, month by month. It is the main entry point for following what is happening in Parliament.

### Navigating between months

The current month is displayed by default. At the bottom of the event list, a **"Load previous month"** button adds events from the previous month directly below those of the current month, separated by a dividing line showing the month name. You can scroll back in time without leaving the page — up to 6 consecutive months.

Beyond 6 months, a **"View earlier months"** link allows you to continue browsing.

As you scroll through the months, the subtitle at the top of the page and the address in the browser bar update automatically to reflect the currently visible month. Filters apply to all loaded months: the counter on each badge adds up all events of that type, and a fully filtered month is hidden.

When viewing a past month, a **"Back to current month"** shortcut appears to return directly to the current month.

### Filtering by event type

A row of clickable badges lets you filter events by category. Each badge shows a coloured icon, a label, and a counter indicating the number of events of that type in the month:

- **All** — all events for the month (total count).
- **Texts** — new texts tabled in Parliament.
- **Decisions** — votes on an entire text, with detailed results.
- **Reports** — reports filed by parliamentary committees.
- **Shuttles** — texts transferred from one chamber to the other. Each card indicates the direction of transfer (e.g. "Assemblée → Sénat").
- **CMP** — convocations of the Joint Committee (Commission mixte paritaire).
- **Cons. const.** — referrals to the Constitutional Council.
- **Enactments** — laws signed and published in the Official Journal (Journal officiel).
- **Decrees** — implementing decrees published by the Government to apply an enacted law.
- **Laws applied** — laws for which all implementing decrees have been published, marking their full entry into force.
- **Motions** — motions of no confidence tabled against the Government.

The active badge is highlighted. On mobile, the row is horizontally scrollable (a gradient on the right edge indicates that there are more filters).

### Reading an event card

Each card shows:

- A **coloured pictogram** indicating the type of event.
- The **type** (New text, Decision, Report, etc.) and the **date**.
- The **author** or **institution** concerned, preceded by a role indicator — **Dep.** for a member of the National Assembly (député), **Sén.** for a senator, **Min.** for a minister — followed by their abbreviated political group.
- The **title** of the associated text or dossier.

Depending on the event type, additional information appears:

- **Decisions**: the card title indicates the **exact subject of the vote** (e.g. "The preliminary rejection motion" or "The text as a whole"). Below, the **procedure type** (e.g. "Ordinary public vote") and the **applicable majority rule** (e.g. "Absolute majority of votes cast") are specified. When data is available, a **vote bar** shows the for/against/abstentions breakdown — hover over it for the detailed figures. A **result badge** (adopted, rejected…) appears below the bar.
- **Reports**: the name of the committee and the origin of the report. When the information is available, the **name of the lead rapporteur** and their political group are shown below the title. If several rapporteurs signed the report, the note "(and colleagues)" is added. For Joint Committee (CMP) reports combining a report from each chamber, the rapporteurs from the **National Assembly** and the **Senate** are indicated separately.
- **Enactments**: a centred celebration icon and the **official law number** (e.g. "Law no. 2025-989") highlighted.
- **Implementing decrees**: the parent law to which the decree relates (with its name and number), the full title of the decree, and an **AI Summary** link pointing directly to the decree.
- **Fully applied law**: a golden trophy icon and the message "All decrees published". The card indicates the number of measures applied, the number of decrees, and the time elapsed since enactment. Two links are provided: **AI Summary** (pointing to the consolidated version of the law) and **Full dossier**.
- **Enactments with direct application**: when a law applies immediately without any decree, a golden **"Direct application"** badge appears below the law number on the enactment card.

When several events of the same type concern the same dossier on the same date, they are grouped into a single card with a counter (×2, ×3…).

### Motions of no confidence

Motions of no confidence receive special treatment. Each card shows:

- The **title** of the related legislative dossier (for example, the finance bill).
- For each motion tabled: the **vote title** (which names the authors of the motion), a **result badge** ("Motion rejected" or "Motion adopted"), and the **vote bar** with detailed results.
- An **AI Summary** link for each motion, which opens directly the summary of the relevant motion text.

### Dossier mode (timeline)

By clicking on the title of a dossier from any card, you access the **full timeline** of that dossier: all the stages it has gone through, from initial tabling to enactment (if applicable). The same filter badges are available, with counters adapted to the dossier's events. A **"← Back to dossier"** link allows you to return to the AI summary page of the dossier.

### AI summary and text availability

On most cards, an **AI Summary** link provides access to the automatic summary of the relevant text. The link pre-selects the correct text in the dropdown menu on the summary page.

When a text has not yet been published by the National Assembly, the AI Summary link is replaced by a **"Not yet published"** indicator accompanied by a **"Full dossier"** link that still allows access to the available information on the dossier (metadata, votes, timeline).

Some cards — notably Constitutional Council votes or decisions without a directly associated text — display only a **"Full dossier"** link, without the "Not yet published" notice (the text is simply not linked to this stage, not unavailable).

## Indicateurs clés

The **Key indicators** (Indicateurs clés) page gives a numerical overview of Parliament's activity since the beginning of the 17th legislature (in progress).

### Summary figures

Three figures at the top of the page summarise overall activity:

- **Total**: the number of legislative dossiers tabled since the beginning of the legislature.
- **This month**: dossiers tabled during the current month.
- **Monthly average**: the average number of dossiers tabled per month, calculated over the last 24 months.

### Activity chart

A bar chart shows the monthly evolution of the number of filings over the last 2 years. It allows you to spot periods of intense activity (parliamentary return, budget review) and quiet periods (summer, recesses).

### Legislative funnel

A visual funnel shows how many texts pass through each major stage of the legislative journey — from tabling to actual application. It gives an at-a-glance idea of the "yield" of the process: out of all dossiers tabled, how many actually result in an applied law?

### Dossier status

Seven boxes distribute all dossiers according to their progress in the legislative journey:

- **In progress** — the text has been tabled and is being studied by one chamber.
- **Adopted by the National Assembly** — the NA has voted, the Senate still has to rule.
- **Adopted by the Senate** — the Senate has voted, the NA still has to rule.
- **Adopted by Parliament** — both chambers have voted the same text, it is awaiting enactment.
- **Enacted** — the President has signed the law, published in the Official Journal.
- **Applied** — the law is in force: either it applies directly (without needing decrees), or all necessary implementing decrees have been published.
- **Rejected** — the text has been abandoned (a new tabling remains possible).

### Process efficiency

Six indicators measure the speed and yield of the legislative process:

- **Enactment rate**: the share of legislative texts that resulted in a law.
- **Application rate**: the share of legislative texts actually applied (direct application or decrees published). This rate uses the same denominator as the enactment rate, allowing you to track progression: X% enacted → Y% applied.
- **Average application delay**: the average time for all implementing decrees of a law to be published. Only laws requiring decrees are taken into account (not laws with direct application).
- **Average enactment delay**: the average time between tabling and publication in the Official Journal.
- **Average delay at the National Assembly**: the average time between tabling at the NA and its adoption by that chamber.
- **Average delay at the Senate**: the same indicator for the upper chamber.

For each delay, the minimum and maximum values are displayed to show the spread.

> To understand precisely how these indicators are calculated, consult the [Methodology](/documentation/methode) page.

### Legislative success by political group

A table at the bottom of the page compares adoption and enactment rates for each political group that has initiated at least 10 dossiers. Groups are organised into three distinct sections:

- **National Assembly** — the political groups of the National Assembly.
- **Senate** — the political groups of the Senate.
- **Government** — all governments of the legislature, aggregated into a single line combining their statistics.

Click on column headers to sort the table by the criterion of your choice.

⚠ This table must be read with caution: an opposition group structurally has fewer texts adopted than a majority group. A low rate does not reflect the quality of parliamentary work, but the arithmetic reality of majority voting.

### Activity by political group

Two horizontal bar charts give an immediate reading of who proposes and who succeeds in getting laws passed.

- **Texts proposed** — the number of legislative dossiers tabled by each group since the beginning of the legislature. An opposition group often tables many texts, even if few of them are adopted: it is a way of participating in public debate.
- **Laws enacted** — the number of laws actually signed by the President that come from each group. The Government naturally dominates here: its bills almost systematically benefit from majority support.

Placed side by side, these two charts allow you to distinguish who *proposes* a lot from who *gets a lot adopted* — two very different realities depending on whether one is in the majority or the opposition.

### Most active parliamentarians

A table lists the 20 deputies and senators most involved in legislative texts, as author or rapporteur of a text. It is sorted by total involvement (authors + rapporteurs combined), and indicates for each one their chamber, political group, and breakdown between the roles of author and rapporteur.

**Search for a parliamentarian** — A search field above the table allows you to find any deputy or senator, even if they do not appear in the top 20. Type a name (or part of a name): the table instantly displays all matching results with their full data.

> This ranking measures formal involvement in texts under study, not necessarily real political influence. A rapporteur is appointed by their committee to technically follow a text; an author is its initiator.

### Filters

Two dropdown menus at the top of the page allow you to refine all indicators:

- **Procedure type** — to isolate government bills, finance laws, private members' bills, etc.
- **Political group** — to see the activity of a particular group.

The **Reset** button clears the filters and returns to the global view.

## Institutional composition

### Overview

The **Composition** page presents a comparative dashboard of the three main institutions: Assemblée nationale, Sénat and Gouvernement. For each, you can see at a glance the number of members, gender parity and average age. An "Explorer" link leads to the detailed page for each institution.

### Detailed pages by institution

Each institution has its own page in the **Legislative bodies** section:

- **[Assemblée nationale](/organes/assemblee)** — 577 deputies elected by direct universal suffrage for 5 years. Full page with voting statistics, individual records, political groups and a table of all deputies.
- **[Sénat](/organes/senat)** — 348 senators elected by indirect universal suffrage for 6 years. Political groups and a table of all senators.
- **[Gouvernement](/organes/gouvernement)** — The Government conducts the policy of the Nation. Members, parity and members table.
- **[Conseil constitutionnel](/organes/conseil-constitutionnel)** — 9 members appointed for 9 years, guardian of the conformity of laws with the Constitution. Educational page explaining its composition, missions and the QPC.

### Assembly mobilisation during votes

On the Assemblée nationale page, you can see how many deputies participate **on average** in each type of vote:

- **Ordinary votes** (day-to-day votes: amendments, articles of law…): average number of voters and non-participants across all votes of the legislature.
- **Important votes** (votes on an entire bill, motions of no confidence): same reading, for these votes considered the most important.

The percentage displayed next to the figure allows you to immediately place mobilisation relative to the 577 seats. In practice, an ordinary vote mobilises about a third of the assembly, while an important vote brings together two thirds.

> **Non-participants** includes deputies absent from the chamber and those who chose not to vote. Institutional non-voters (President of the NA, session chairpersons, Government members) are excluded from the calculation — they are not counted as absent.

For details on the six types of votes and their majority rules, consult the [Methodology](/documentation/methode#types-de-scrutins) page.

### Individual participation and cohesion records

The Assemblée nationale page also shows **individual records**: the deputy with the highest and lowest participation in votes (all votes, then important votes only), as well as the deputy most and least aligned with their political group. These figures allow you to identify the extremes and grasp the extent of differences in voting behaviour among elected representatives.

### Detailed table of deputies

The Assemblée nationale page also offers a table listing each deputy with their individual indicators:

- Their **participation rate** in votes (all votes and important votes), accompanied by the detail **"X / Y"**: X votes cast out of Y votes held during their term.
- Their **cohesion rate** with their political group.

These rates are calculated over the **actual term period**: a representative who arrived mid-legislature (by-election) or who temporarily left their seat (for a ministerial post, for example) is only evaluated on the votes during which they were actually in office.

## Dossiers législatifs

The **All texts** page lists all legislative dossiers tabled during the current legislature.

### Reading a dossier card

Each dossier is presented as a compact row structured in three levels:

1. A **coloured bullet** indicates the status at a glance (gold = applied, green = enacted, purple = adopted, red = rejected, orange = under review), followed by the **exact status** and the **duration** ("tabled X days ago" or "enacted in X days").
2. The **title** of the text.
3. **Metadata**: the procedure type (with explanatory tooltip if available), the **role and name of the author** (Deputy, Senator or Minister + name), their **political group**, and the **tabling date**. If no published text is available, the note "No text" or "Text not yet published" appears at the end of the row. A **LoiClair dossier with AI summary** button provides direct access to the dossier summary page.

### Filters and search

The **search bar** spans the full width at the top of the filter area and lets you search for a dossier by keyword in the title or by author name.

Below it, a **Filters** button opens a side panel (on the right on desktop, from the bottom on mobile) with five available filters:

- **Procedure type** — to isolate a particular type of text.
- **Status** — to see only applied, enacted, in progress, rejected texts, etc. The **Applied** status means that the law has been enacted and its implementing decrees are published (or that it has direct application).
- **Political group** — to filter by initiating group.
- **Theme** — to display only dossiers on a given subject (health, education, transport, etc.). Themes are assigned automatically from the title of each dossier (see [Methodology — Thematic classification](/documentation/methode#classification-thematique)).
- **Age** — to filter by filing date (less than 6 months, 6 months to 1 year, more than 1 year).

A **counter badge** on the button shows how many filters are active. Selected filters appear as **chips** next to the button: each chip shows the filter label and an **×** to remove it with one click. The **Reset** button clears all filters at once.

The pagination at the bottom of the page allows you to navigate between results (10 dossiers per page).

### AI summary

On each card, a **LoiClair dossier with AI summary** button gives access to the dossier page, which includes an explanation of the text generated automatically by artificial intelligence, in plain language and without legal jargon, as well as vote results and dossier metadata. This button is always accessible, even when the text has not yet been published.

On the dossier page, the layout is organised from top to bottom to go from the essential to the detailed:

- The **dossier title** is displayed at the top of the page on a banner labelled "LoiClair Dossier".
- The **AI summary**, signalled by a ✨ icon and the "AI Summary" label, is presented in a card with a glowing border. It is structured in **3 sections**: *What this text says* (2-3 simple sentences in plain language), *What changes concretely* (3-5 action points with teal badges), and *Key takeaway* (a single key sentence highlighted in a coloured box). The AI addresses the reader informally to make the explanation more natural and accessible.
- At the bottom of the card, the **summarised text** is indicated with a link **"Read the text"** to the official document and a **"Change"** button to select another text. Texts are grouped by legislative stage (National Assembly, Senate, CMP, Enacted…) and sorted by date. For enacted laws, the menu also includes **implementing decrees** and the **consolidated version**.
- An animated gold link **"Discuss this text with the AI"** allows you to open an in-depth discussion on the text via Perplexity, with the legislative context pre-filled.
- If the selected text has not yet been published, a message clearly indicates this and the "Change" button remains accessible to select another text.
- An **"About this law"** section presents the dossier metadata as a structured grid: **status** (Enacted, In progress, Rejected…), **procedure type** (with explanatory tooltip), **author** (role and name), **political group**, **tabling date**, and **links to official sources** (National Assembly, Senate, Légifrance). A link to the **news feed** for the dossier is available at the bottom of this section.
- A **vertical timeline** traces the legislative journey of the dossier step by step (Tabling → National Assembly → Senate → CMP → Constitutional Council → Enacted…), with the **date** of each stage and the **duration in days** between two stages. The current stage is signalled by an animated dot and the note "in progress for X days" in orange. Stages not yet reached appear as dotted lines. Each stage that has associated texts includes an **accordion**: by clicking, you see the list of available texts (tabled text, committee text, text adopted in plenary…) and can select them to get the AI summary. The **Enacted** stage has a ✓ icon and displays in green the **total duration** of the journey. After enactment, the timeline displays the **implementing decrees** and the law's **application status**.

At the bottom of each summary page, a **Transparency** section indicates that the content was generated by artificial intelligence, with a link to the [AI Act Compliance](/documentation/conformite-ia) page. By expanding the **Generation parameters**, you can view all the settings used: the model name, the provider, the temperature (which controls the AI's creativity), the maximum number of tokens produced, and the system prompt — that is, the exact instructions given to the AI to produce the summary.

> During generation, an **"analysis in progress…"** message is displayed next to the "AI Summary" title. If generation fails, an error message appears with a **Retry →** button to restart the request without reloading the page.

## Processus législatif

The **Legislative process** (Processus législatif) page is an educational resource that explains how a law is created in France, from A to Z.

### Key actors in the legislative process

A grid of illustrated cards presents the 5 key institutions, each with a photo of its building and a summary of its role. Click on a card to view the full description in a modal:

- **The National Assembly (Assemblée nationale)** — 577 deputies, the lower chamber of Parliament.
- **The Senate (Sénat)** — 348 senators, the upper chamber of Parliament.
- **The Government (Gouvernement)** — may table government bills and engage its responsibility.
- **The President of the Republic** — enacts laws and may refer matters to the Constitutional Council.
- **The Constitutional Council (Conseil constitutionnel)** — verifies the conformity of laws with the Constitution.

### The stages of a law

An interactive vertical stepper presents the 8 stages that an ordinary legislative text goes through. Click on each step to see the details:

1. Tabling of the text
2. Committee examination
3. Plenary debate (1st chamber)
4. Shuttle between the two chambers (or Joint Committee)
5. Final vote
6. Constitutional review (if referred)
7. Enactment and publication in the Official Journal
8. Implementing decrees — the law can only take concrete effect once the decrees are published by the Government

> On average, an ordinary law takes between 6 and 12 months to complete this journey.

**What is the Joint Committee (Commission mixte paritaire — CMP)?**
In France, a law must be voted in identical terms by the National Assembly and the Senate. When the two chambers cannot reach agreement after several readings, the Prime Minister — or the presidents of the two assemblies for a parliamentary initiative law — may convene a Joint Committee. Its **purpose**: to find a compromise text acceptable to both chambers, rather than prolonging the shuttle indefinitely. Its **composition**: 7 deputies and 7 senators, chosen to reflect the political diversity of each assembly. If the CMP succeeds in drafting a common text, it is submitted to a vote of both chambers. If it fails, the National Assembly has the final say and may adopt the text alone. In the news feed, the convening of a CMP appears as a distinct card.

**Why multiple referrals to the Constitutional Council for the same law?**
Article 61 of the Constitution allows 60 deputies *or* 60 senators to refer a matter to the Constitutional Council before a law is enacted. Several political groups may file separate referrals on the same day — or a few days apart — for practical and political reasons: each group drafts its own legal arguments, sometimes challenges different articles, and may prefer not to sign the same document as its political adversaries, even if they oppose the same text. In the news feed, each referral therefore appears as a distinct card.

## Types de textes législatifs

The **Text types** (Types de textes) page explains the different categories of texts produced by Parliament and the Government. It is organized in two sections.

### Parliamentary texts

Texts initiated or produced by Parliament (National Assembly / Senate):

- **Private member's bill (Proposition de loi)** — tabled by one or more parliamentarians.
- **Organic private member's bill (Proposition de loi organique)** — a law that specifies the conditions of application of the Constitution; enhanced procedure.
- **Amendment (Amendement)** — a modification proposed on a text under discussion.
- **Report (Rapport)** — an analytical document produced by a parliamentary committee.
- **Resolution (Résolution)** — a text expressing Parliament's position, without the force of law.
- **Other** — opinions, impact assessments, international agreements.

### Government texts

Texts initiated by the Government or executive branch:

- **Government bill (Projet de loi)** — tabled by the government.
- **Ordinance (Ordonnance)** — a measure taken by the Government with Parliament's authorization, in a domain normally reserved for law.
- **Decree (Décret)** — a regulatory text specifying the implementation details of a law, without going through Parliament.

## Une idée ? Un problème ?

An **Idea? Problem?** button is visible at the bottom right of each page. By clicking on it, you can choose between two actions:

### Report a problem

Report any malfunction observed on the site:

- **incorrect data** (figure, name, date, etc.),
- a **display problem** (broken layout, missing element),
- a **broken link**,
- an **incorrect AI summary** (factual error in a generated summary),
- or **any other issue**.

### Suggest an idea

Suggest improvements or new features:

- a **new feature** you would like to see on LoiClair,
- an **improvement** to an existing feature,
- or **any other idea**.

In both cases, a ticket is automatically created on LoiClair's public GitHub repository after submission. A link is provided for you to track progress. The email address is optional — it is only used if the team needs to contact you.

## Documentation

The **Documentation** section, accessible from the left menu, brings together four complementary resources:

- **User guide** — you are here.
- **Glossary** — definitions of the legislative terms used on LoiClair: dossier statuses, procedure types, legislative stages. The glossary is fed directly from the database — terms appear automatically as new procedures are referenced.
- **Methodology** — detailed explanation of each indicator displayed on the site: calculation formula, variables included and excluded, data source, interpretation limits.
- **AI Act Compliance** — explanation of the European regulation on artificial intelligence (EU Regulation 2024/1689), LoiClair's role as a deployer, and the concrete measures put in place to comply with Article 50: visible notice on each summary, publication of the system prompt, machine-readable marking, and explicit neutrality in the instructions given to the AI.

## Release notes

The **Release notes** page, accessible from the site's footer, traces the complete history of LoiClair's developments. Each week is summarised with new features, modifications and corrections made. Sections are expandable: the most recent week is open by default, previous ones can be consulted with a single click. The content is displayed in the selected language (French or English).
